Kimsuky - APT43 Disclaimer

Please note that the cyber espionage activities of Kimsuky, also known as APT43, are associated with the North Korean government. This document will discuss the operation from the perspective of South Korea, examining both offensive and defensive considerations in response to this threat.

Kimsuky - APT43 Overview

Kimsuky, also known as APT43, Thallium, Black Banshee, Velvet Chollima, and Emerald Sleet, is a North Korean threat actor group associated with the North Korean government. Active since at least 2012 and first identified in 2013 by Kaspersky researchers, Kimsuky has been involved in cyber espionage targeting government, research, and defense sectors in countries including South Korea, the United States, and Japan. The group primarily targets government agencies, think tanks, media, and nuclear sectors, focusing on gathering intelligence. Kimsuky is believed to have connections to other North Korean APT groups such as Lazarus and APT37 (Reaper).

Offensive Cyber Strategy

Phishing Campaigns - Initial Access Tool

Phishing campaigns are essential for initiating access in cyber espionage. Kimsuky has used tailored spear-phishing attacks to exploit vulnerabilities in target organizations, establishing footholds for further data gathering. They create spoofed and fraudulent personas for social engineering use, masquerade as key individuals in their target area, and leverage stolen personally identifiable information (PII) to create accounts and register domains.

Malware Implants - Persistence and Data Collection

Kimsuky deploys custom malware such as BabyShark, Gold Dragon, and SWEETDROP, designed to maintain persistence within networks, conduct reconnaissance, and exfiltrate sensitive information to North Korean command centers. BabyShark malware is used to collect data and is often delivered through spear-phishing emails. Gold Dragon is a data-gathering tool that can perform basic reconnaissance, data extraction, and download additional components from its command and control (C2) server, ensuring a lasting presence on victims' systems. SWEETDROP is a malware dropper that collects basic system information.

Exploited Vulnerabilities

  • CVE-2017-0199: Exploited to deliver BabyShark malware via malicious Microsoft Office documents.
  • HWP Exploits: Hangul Word Processor files widely used in South Korea.
  • CVE-2015-2545: Microsoft Office vulnerability allowing remote code execution through EPS image files.
  • CVE-2019-0604: Microsoft SharePoint vulnerability to execute arbitrary code.

Notable Attacks

UN Security Council Attack (2020)

  • Overview: Between March and April 2020, the Kimsuky group attempted to compromise the accounts of several UN officials through spear-phishing, aiming to access or spread malware onto the victims' systems.
  • Techniques: The group used spear-phishing emails and messages, posing as UN security alerts or requests from journalists for interviews, to lure officials into accessing phishing pages. These attempts were made via email and communication platforms such as WhatsApp.

Operation Stolen Pencil (December 2018)

  • Overview: This operation targeted academia and research institutions, primarily in regions with ties to South Korean policy matters.
  • Techniques: Kimsuky used spear-phishing emails to lure victims to websites that appeared to be legitimate academic organizations. Victims were then prompted to install a malicious Google Chrome extension capable of stealing cookies and passwords from the browser, process memory, network sniffing, and keylogging. The specific goals of this attack remain unknown.

Defensive Cyber Strategy

Detection Strategies

To effectively defend against Kimsuky's activities, understanding their methods is crucial. This includes familiarity with their common attack vectors (spear-phishing, malicious Chrome extensions, exploiting known vulnerabilities, watering hole attacks), file types (Microsoft Word, Hangul Word Processor files), and threat categories (trojan, downloader, dropper). Recommended strategies include:

  • Being vigilant about domains previously used by Kimsuky to carry out objectives.
  • Educating and training staff to recognize phishing emails and malicious attachments.
  • Monitoring for suspicious activity within networks.
  • Utilizing advanced security software and tools such as Next-Generation Firewalls (NGFW), Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) systems, and Extended Detection and Response (XDR) solutions.
  • Collaborating with other organizations to share information on threats and incidents.

Next-Generation Firewalls (NGFW) - Network Security Tool

NGFWs provide enhanced intrusion detection and prevention capabilities, identifying suspicious traffic and blocking known indicators of compromise, making them essential for defending against advanced persistent threats like Kimsuky.

Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) - Centralized Security Platform

SIEM solutions like Splunk or Sentinel aggregate and analyze security logs, enabling rapid detection and response to potential breaches and anomalies within the network.

Extended Detection and Response (XDR) - Endpoint Security Solution

XDR platforms provide comprehensive endpoint security, ensuring quick identification and response to threats across all entry points, enhancing the overall security posture.
