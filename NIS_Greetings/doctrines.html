US Federal Doctrine

Cybersecurity Enhancement Act of 7/16/2002 (US Congress, Sec. 101A - Sec. 206, https://www.congress.gov/bill/107th-congress/house-bill/3482)

Promotes Research and Development (RnD) for critical infrastructure security: The act incentivizes investment on behalf of federal agencies and private sector companies in cutting-edge technologies that would serve to protect energy grids, financial systems, and transportation networks from cyber threats. This act appropriates money for long-term research projects directed towards the creation of more advanced cyber defense systems, better encryption techniques, and monitoring tools to trace and respond to threats in real time. Its objective is to give security and resilience to these infrastructures so that they may be capable of resisting all kinds of cyber threats, whether from outside or inside.

Fosters collaboration between government agencies, private companies, and academic institutions: The act aims at bringing together the federal government, states, and local government agencies, industries, and universities to partner in developing one cybersecurity strategy. It pools resources, expertise, and talents for addressing the tough area of cybersecurity challenges faced by the nation. For example, government agencies provide regulatory guidance and oversight while academic institutions can provide research, training, and the development of the next generation of cybersecurity professionals. In such cases, where these are private sector companies, especially with involvements in technology and infrastructure, sharing their data and innovations, the same can be made available in developing policies and solutions on cybersecurity.

Penalties: fines depend on severity, up to 20 years in prison

Cybersecurity Labeling for Internet of Things (IoT) Devices of 5/12/2021 (NIST, https://www.nist.gov/itl/executive-order-14028-improving-nations-cybersecurity/cybersecurity-labeling-consumers-0)

A cybersecurity label on IoT devices to assist consumers in making informed purchasing decisions: The initiative is introducing a standardized label on IoT devices that will provide critical information to consumers on the product's security features. This is a label intended for buyers to be informed of just how secure a device is to help understand its vulnerabilities. In particular, it will include but is not limited to the following: whether the device has encryption built into it, whether it avails software updating in a secure way, or has other data protection protocols. The labeling system is designed to empower users with transparency to make safer choices by trying to make the IoE devices safer, mandated for homes, businesses, and industries which traditionally have been prime targets for cyber-attacks.

Penalties: fines depend on severity, FTC will take appropriate action

Federal Cybersecurity Vulnerability Reduction Act of 8/22/2023 (US Congress, Sec. 1- Sec. 2, https://www.congress.gov/bill/118th-congress/house-bill/5255/text)

Requires federal contractors to implement vulnerability disclosure policies: The act requires that every federal contractor implement a policy for the disclosure of vulnerabilities, enabling security researchers and third parties to report identified weaknesses within the contractor's systems. Accordingly, it is easier and less dangerous for third parties to submit reports on potential weaknesses, assisting the contractor in finding and remediating vulnerabilities before these become exploited. The procedures for handling reported issues should be anthropologically stated, right from handling to resolution. This procedure ensures timely mitigation against any security flaws in systems connected with federal operations to swiftly mitigate cyberattacks.

Ensures that contractors report system vulnerabilities to the government to preserve federal operations: The act empowers the government to patch up and fix security weaknesses before they can be used by cyber threats. Holding contractors responsible for identifying and communicating such issues in due time empowers security for the federal systems to continue running core functions of the government securely.

Penalties: fines depend on severity, loss of contract

US State-level Doctrine

Virginia Consumer Data Protection Act (CDPA) 1/1/2021 (Bloomberg, https://pro.bloomberglaw.com/insights/privacy/virginia-consumer-data-protection-act-vcdpa/#what-is-the-vcdpa)

Provides Virginians finer control over their personal data: The CDPA grants more rights to Virginians when it comes to their data. This bill grants Virginians rights to understand how businesses collect, process, and share their data. This includes rights enabling an individual to take greater control over the information held about them so that businesses are more transparent. One of the key focuses of the law is privacy by design, meaning a company must create policies to protect personal data from its conception, rather than an afterthought. Consequently, it ensures that the highest standards with regards to data management are met by businesses, thereby giving the individual every opportunity to take responsibility for their privacy.

Virginians can request access to their personal data: Under the CDPA, Virginia residents have the right to make requests to access their personal data held by companies, similar to the rights outlined in other major privacy laws such as the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) and the EU’s GDPR. Individuals can ask businesses to provide detailed information on what data is being collected about them, how it is being used, and with whom it is shared. Virginians also have the right to rectification if their data is incorrect, deletion of the data if not needed anymore, and a portable copy of the personal data. Most importantly, residents have the privilege of opting out of certain kinds of data processing, such as targeted advertising, the sale of personal data, or profiling for commercial purposes. This offers an opportunity for Virginians to learn how their data is used in the digital economy.

Businesses must handle personal data more carefully: It would impose a legal requirement on businesses to make sure Virginians' personal data is well protected with appropriate data security practices. Firms should be more transparent regarding the collection and processing of data, with proper legal grounds for sensitive personal data collection. An organization should review the processes that involve data processing in order to ensure that they meet the requirements imposed by the CDPA, particularly when sensitive data such as race, health information, sexual orientation, and geolocation are being collected. Moreover, an organization shall conduct a data protection impact assessment with regard to those activities of data processing that are likely to involve a high level of risk, such as processing sensitive information or disclosing data on large scales. This helps reduce the risks of data breaches or misuse of personal data, ensuring that consumers' rights are prioritized over business interests.

Penalties: $7500 fine

Georgia Personal Identity Protection Act of 2007 (CyberSec Ops Division, https://www.usg.edu/cybersecurity/cyberlaw_and_legislation)

Requires notification to Georgia residents in case of a data breach: The Georgia Personal Identity Protection Act compels any business or organization that maintains personal data of a resident in Georgia, upon breach, to notify those concerned persons immediately. A breach of data can be defined as unauthorized access and exposure or theft of sensitive personal information that may be used for the purposes of identity fraud or economic loss. The law provides that, in the case of immediate notification, a person is kept informed of the risks their personal data may be exposed to and can therefore easily take swift action to protect themselves by way of password changes, monitoring of financial accounts, or freezing of credit reports. The notification needed must also include the type of breach that has occurred, the type of data affected, and what planning should be done by the individual in mitigating the risk involved. Timely notification can reduce the overall impact of data breaches while helping restore consumer trust in enterprise businesses.

Protects sensitive personal data: The Georgia Personal Identity Protection Act is designed to safeguard a range of sensitive personal information, including Social Security numbers, driver’s license numbers, financial account details (such as bank account or credit card numbers), and online credentials like usernames, passwords, and answers to security questions. This sensitive data, if exposed, could be used for fraudulent purposes, including identity theft or unauthorized financial transactions. The law requires businesses to implement appropriate security measures to protect this data from unauthorized access or disclosure. Additionally, companies must ensure that any third parties with access to such data also adhere to strict data protection policies. By enforcing stringent security standards, the law aims to reduce the risk of data breaches and ensure that businesses take proactive steps to protect consumer information.

Penalties: $500 per record

NATO-EU Doctrine

European Union - NIS Directive (Directive on Security of Network and Information Systems)

Purpose and Scope: The NIS Directive establishes a common level of security for network and information systems across the EU. It aims to protect critical infrastructure and essential digital services from cyber threats. The directive applies to two main groups:

  • Operators of Essential Services (OES): Critical sectors like energy, transport, banking, and healthcare.
  • Relevant Digital Service Providers (RDSPs): Online marketplaces, search engines, and cloud computing services.

Key Requirements:
  • Security Measures: Covered entities must implement technical and organizational measures like anti-malware, multi-factor authentication, encryption, and access controls.
  • Incident Reporting: Organizations must report significant cybersecurity incidents to national authorities to facilitate coordinated responses.
  • National Strategies: Each EU member state must adopt a national cybersecurity strategy with defined strategic goals and policies.

Estonia's Cybersecurity Act (2018)

Purpose and Scope: The Cybersecurity Act creates a legal framework for ensuring cybersecurity in Estonia, applying to essential service operators, digital service providers, and government authorities.

Key Provisions:

  • Cybersecurity Measures: Entities must implement anti-malware solutions, access controls, encryption, and regular security assessments.
  • Incident Reporting: Organizations must report incidents to the Estonian Information System Authority (RIA) to enable coordinated responses.
  • Powers of the RIA: The RIA has the authority to restrict access to systems, conduct supervisory proceedings, and issue fines for non-compliance.

Germany's IT Security Act 2.0 (2021)

Purpose and Scope: This act strengthens Germany's cybersecurity, applying to critical infrastructure operators, digital service providers, and federal authorities.

Key Provisions:

  • Expanded Powers for BSI: The Federal Office for Information Security (BSI) gains authority to detect vulnerabilities and defend against cyber attacks.
  • Critical Infrastructure Protection: New sectors, like waste management, are now part of critical infrastructure, and operators must implement stricter security measures.
  • Cybersecurity in Mobile Networks: Regulations ensure security in 5G networks and prohibit components that threaten public order.
  • Consumer Protection: BSI serves as an advisory body on IT security issues and introduces a uniform IT Security Mark for secure products.
  • Incident Reporting: Companies must report significant incidents to BSI promptly, including personal data breaches.
