UNC4899

Kiara Catalano

Introduction

UNC4899 is a newly identified threat actor attributed to North Korea’s Reconnaissance General Bureau. This actor successfully hacked into JumpCloud, a U.S.-based IT company whose software serves over 180,000 organizations and more than 5,000 customers globally. As the U.S. is a NATO ally to South Korea, this attack has implications for South Korea’s national cybersecurity strategy. The attack was traced to Pyongyang through a compromised IP address, revealing links to a North Korean subnet (175.45.178.0/24). Pyongyang has historically used cyber operations, including cryptocurrency theft, to fund its nuclear weapons program and gather intelligence.

Description of Cyber Operation

The UNC4899 operation began with a sophisticated spearphishing campaign on June 22, 2023, granting unauthorized access to five customers and ten systems. Hackers compromised JumpCloud systems, inserting a malicious Ruby script, init.rb, into the command framework, triggering a software supply chain attack. This script was activated on June 27, 2023, specifically targeting four systems running macOS Venture versions 13.3 or 13.4.1.

Second-Stage Payload - FULLHOUSE.DOORED

Following the initial activation, a second-stage payload named FULLHOUSE.DOORED deployed additional malware. FULLHOUSE.DOORED, a C/C++ backdoor, supports HTTP-based communication and can execute shell commands, transfer files, manage files, and inject processes.

Additional Malware - STRATOFEAR and TIEDYE

After executing FULLHOUSE.DOORED, UNC4899 deployed two additional malware variants: STRATOFEAR and TIEDYE. STRATOFEAR gathers system information and retrieves or executes modules from a remote server or disk, while TIEDYE communicates with a remote server, executing commands, gathering basic system information, and managing shell commands. Prior payloads were removed from the systems to avoid detection and reduce suspicion.

Legal Issues Governing the Authorized Conduct of Cyber Operations

South Korea – Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA)

PIPA defines principles for protecting personal data, rights of individuals, and the state’s obligations. Key Articles include:

  • Article 3: Defines data protection requirements such as accurate data processing, purpose limitation, and security safeguards.
  • Article 4: Grants individuals rights to be informed, consent, access, correct, and delete their data.
  • Article 5: Obligates the government to implement policies supporting privacy and self-regulation.
  • Articles 15-22: Restricts personal information collection, use, and third-party sharing to cases with user consent or legal necessity.
  • Articles 29-34: Outlines data protection measures like privacy risk assessments and breach notifications.
  • Penalty Provisions: Violations can result in up to 10 years of imprisonment or a 100 million won fine, depending on severity.

Based on PIPA, the JumpCloud hack is illegal, involving unauthorized data collection and misuse, potentially leading to severe penalties if prosecuted in South Korea.

U.S. – Cyber Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA)

The CFAA prohibits unauthorized computer access and damage. Key sections include:

  • Section (a)(1), (a)(2): Prohibits unauthorized access to protected computers.
  • Section (a)(5): Prohibits knowingly sending code or commands that cause damage.
  • Section (a)(6): Prohibits trafficking in access information to allow unauthorized access.
  • Section (a)(7): Prohibits using threats to extort by promising unauthorized access.

Penalties range from 1 to 20 years of imprisonment, depending on the severity, with UNC4899’s actions falling under unauthorized access and data collection, punishable up to 10 years if prosecuted in the U.S.

Summary of Tools, Techniques, Technology, and Data Used

  • Social Engineering: Influences actions using reciprocity, authority, and other principles to deceive users.
  • Malicious Scripts and Payloads: Inject harmful code for monitoring, data exfiltration, and system exploitation.
  • Code Injection: Alters program execution by embedding malicious code.
  • Server Supply Chain Attacks: Targets third-party suppliers to compromise systems.
  • Evasion Techniques: Removes payloads to avoid detection and alerting system administrators.

Defensive Cyber Strategy

Recommended Tool/Weapon

Defensive tools include Next-Generation Firewalls (NGFW), Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) platforms, and Extended Detection and Response (XDR) systems.

Purpose and Summary of Each Cyber Tool/Weapon

  • NGFW: Detects and prevents network intrusions, monitors for suspicious activity, and blocks malicious IPs and domains.
  • SIEM Platform: Centralizes log and event data, aiding in threat detection, incident response, and compliance.
  • XDR Solution: Enhances endpoint security, monitoring across cloud and network environments for quick threat response.

Legal Issues Governing the Authorized Conduct of Cyber Operations

South Korea's defensive cybersecurity framework includes laws like the Information and Communications Network Act (ICNA) and Critical Information Infrastructure Protection Act (CIIPA), which mandate digital asset protection and outline penalties for non-compliance. Violations can result in fines or imprisonment depending on the severity.

Summary of Defensive Tools, Techniques, Technology, and Data

  • Next-Generation Firewall: Provides network security with intrusion detection and prevention capabilities.
  • SIEM Platform: Collects and aggregates data for efficient threat detection and response.
  • XDR Solution: Enhances endpoint visibility and security, facilitating rapid response and protection across the network.
